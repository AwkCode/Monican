## The Goal of a Discovery Call

You are NOT selling on this call. You are doing three things:
1. Understanding their workflow pain badly enough to describe it back to them better than they can
2. Qualifying whether they're a real prospect (can pay, has decision authority, has a solvable problem)
3. Earning the right to propose a paid engagement

A good discovery call ends with: "Can I put together a short proposal based on what you just told me?"

---

## Before the Call (5 minutes of prep)

- Google the company. Note headcount, industry, what they sell, how long they've been around.
- Check their website for: contact forms, help pages, pricing pages, any obvious manual processes.
- Check LinkedIn for the person you're meeting. Note their role, how long they've been there, mutual connections.
- Have a blank copy of the Workflow Audit Intake (below) ready to fill in during the call.

## Call Structure (30 minutes)

### 1. OPEN — Build rapport, set the frame (3 min)

**Script:**
> "Hey [Name], thanks for taking the time. Before we jump in — I help small businesses figure out where AI can actually save them time. Not the hype stuff, the practical stuff. I'd love to just learn about how your team operates and see if there's anything obvious I can help with. Sound good?"

**Why this works:** You're not pitching. You're asking permission to listen. Every SMB owner has been pitched to death. This is different.

**If they ask about you first:**
> "Quick version — I build AI workflows and agents for small businesses. I built a full operations platform for a venue in Boston using AI tools, and now I help other businesses do similar things. But honestly, the best way for me to know if I can help you is to understand what your day-to-day looks like."

---

### 2. UNDERSTAND THEIR WORLD — Map the business (7 min)

These questions establish context. Don't skip them — you need this information to make smart recommendations later.

| Question | What you're really learning |
|----------|---------------------------|
| "Walk me through a typical week for you. What takes up most of your time?" | Their perception of where time goes |
| "How many people are on your team, and what does each person spend most of their day doing?" | Headcount, roles, potential automation targets |
| "What tools and software does your team use every day?" | Tech stack — determines what's possible |
| "When a new [lead/client/order] comes in, what happens? Walk me through the steps." | The core workflow — this is where the money is |
| "Is there anything your team does that feels like it should be automated but isn't?" | Their self-diagnosis (often wrong, but revealing) |

**Tip:** When they describe a process, ask "and then what happens?" at least twice. The real pain is usually 2-3 steps deeper than what they first describe.

---

### 3. FIND THE PAIN — Identify the costly workflows (10 min)

Now you're drilling into specific processes. You're looking for workflows that are:
- **Repetitive** (same steps, every time)
- **Time-consuming** (multiple hours per week per person)
- **Error-prone** (humans make mistakes here regularly)
- **Bottlenecked** (one person is the chokepoint)

| Question | What you're really learning |
|----------|---------------------------|
| "You mentioned [process]. How many times per week does that happen?" | Volume — is this worth automating? |
| "How long does it take each time?" | Time cost — this becomes your ROI number |
| "Who does it? Is it always the same person?" | Dependency risk + who feels the pain most |
| "What happens when it goes wrong? How often does that happen?" | Error cost — another ROI lever |
| "Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?" | Past attempts tell you about buy-in and budget |
| "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about your operations, what would it be?" | Their #1 priority — lead your proposal with this |

**The money question (ask this every time):**
> "If I'm hearing you right, [person] spends about [X hours/week] on [process], and when it breaks, [consequence]. Is that roughly right?"

**Why this matters:** You're restating their pain in concrete terms. When they say "yes, exactly," you've earned credibility. And you now have the ROI math: X hours/week x hourly cost = what they're spending on this problem.

---

### 4. QUALIFY — Can this become a paying engagement? (5 min)

You need to know three things before you propose anything:

**Budget signal:**
> "If there was a way to cut that [X hours/week] down to [fraction], and it cost you somewhere in the range of [$range/month], is that the kind of investment your team would consider?"

Don't ask "what's your budget?" — they'll lowball you or say they don't have one. Instead, float a range and watch their reaction.

**Decision authority:**
> "If we put something together that made sense, who else would need to sign off on it?"

If the answer is "just me" — great. If it's "I'd need to run it by my partner/boss" — you need that person on the next call.

**Timeline:**
> "Is this something you'd want to move on soon, or more of a down-the-road thing?"

"Soon" = real prospect. "Down the road" = nurture, don't chase.

---

### 5. CLOSE — Set the next step (5 min)

**If they're qualified and interested:**
> "Here's what I'd like to do. I'll put together a short proposal based on everything you told me — the specific workflows I think we can improve, what tools I'd recommend, and what it would cost. I can have that to you by [date]. Does [day] work for a 15-minute follow-up to walk through it?"

**If they're interested but not qualified (no budget, no authority, no urgency):**
> "This was really helpful. I think there's a lot we could do here. Let me send you a quick summary of what I heard and some ideas. When the timing is right, we can pick this back up."

**If they're not a fit:**
> "Honestly, based on what you described, I don't think AI automation is the right move for you right now. [Brief reason.] But if anything changes, I'd love to revisit."

**Always end with:**
> "One more thing — is there anyone else you know who deals with similar operational headaches? I'm always looking to have more of these conversations."

## Common Objections and How to Handle Them

**"We already tried AI and it didn't work."**
> "What did you try? ... That makes sense — [tool/approach] is great for [X] but probably wasn't the right fit for [their specific problem]. What I do is different — I start with the workflow, not the tool. The tool is just the last step."

**"I don't have budget for this."**
> "Totally fair. Let me ask you this — how much is [person] making per hour to do [manual task] right now? If we can cut that time by 80%, the math usually works out to paying for itself in the first month."

**"We're too small for AI."**
> "Actually, small teams get the most out of this. You don't have a big IT department to build things, which means your people are doing work by hand that could be automated. That's exactly where I help."

**"Can't you just tell me what to do and I'll figure it out?"**
> "I can point you in the right direction for sure. But the implementation is where 90% of people get stuck. The value isn't knowing which tool to use — it's getting it wired into your actual workflows so your team actually uses it."

**"How do I know this will actually work?"**
> "Fair question. Here's what I'd suggest — let me do a small pilot on one workflow. If it saves you the time I think it will, we expand. If it doesn't, you're out nothing. I'm not here to sell you a year-long contract."

## After the Call (Within 24 Hours)

1. Send a follow-up email summarizing the 2-3 workflows you discussed and the time/cost impact
2. If proposing: send the proposal within 48 hours (urgency matters)
3. Add them to your CRM/tracker with next action and date
4. If they mentioned a referral: follow up on that specifically
5. Connect on LinkedIn if you haven't already

---

## Red Flags — Walk Away From These

- They want you to "prove it" with weeks of free work before committing
- The decision maker won't get on a call
- They describe their problem as "we just need someone to figure out AI for us" with zero specificity
- They're shopping 5+ vendors for the cheapest option
- Their business model depends on something AI can't do (physical labor, regulated processes that require human sign-off)
